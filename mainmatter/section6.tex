\section{Results and Discussion}
Training is performed on one NVIDIA-A30 GPU. Due to long training times and therefore expensive training, only specific bottleneck dimensions are analyzed. One trained model and its resulting mutual information estimates are referred to as one sample.

For both inference cases, after 250 epochs every observed model has reached its best validation performance, begins to overfit and plateaus in both training and validation losses. This raises the question of how to make a principled choice of epoch to use for the evaluation of mutual information. Three possible selection rules are tested:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item   $5\%$ rule: The model for which training and validation loss are last within $5\%$ of each other.
    \item   Best validation rule: The model with the best validation performance.
    \item   Last epoch rule: The last epoch the model was trained on before training ends.
\end{enumerate}
These rules are justified as follows: The $5\%$ rule allows to choose a model based on training performance while preventing overfitting. The exact value of allowed relative difference is of course an additional hyperparameter. The best validation rule is standard practice in choosing a model after training and the hope is that such a model performs best on the test set as well. The last epoch rule can be justified retrospectively: Because the training process for these architectures is quite unstable before it reaches its plateau, with individual models performing significantly better or worse on the validation set, it allows to choose the model from the most stable part of the training process. When analyzing the conditional entropy and mutual information curves for different $N_B$ in the following, the dimension for which information saturation is reached is referred to as the optimal dimension, though a truly optimal representation is of course never achieved.

The final estimates on the conditional entropy as well as the mutual information are done on the held-out test set consisting of $10\%$ of the total dataset ($500$ samples), according to the estimate of mutual information and conditional entropy defined in section~\ref{subsubsec:mi_estimate}.
\begin{align}
    \hat{\mathbb{H}}_C &= -\E_{p(\theta,s(x))} \bigl[\log q_\phi (\theta|s(x))\bigr]~\label{eq:final_ce_estimate} \\
    \hat{\mathbb{I}} &= -\E_{p(\theta,s(x))} \bigl[\log q_\phi (\theta|s(x)) - p(\theta)\bigr],~\label{eq:final_mi_estimate}
\end{align}
where the previously established notation is used. If the prior is modelled separately, it is replaced with $q_\psi(\theta)$.

Compared to the rest of training, these estimates are inexpensive to evaluate, so it is done every epoch, which enables the visualization of CE and MI during training. For the inference of the neutral hydrogen fraction, the separate estimate of the prior leads to an additional uncertainty in the final MI estimate. For this reason, the interpretation of results is preferably done on the estimates of CE.

Two sources of error in the final estimates are considered. The error of the individual model, stemming from the variability of log probability values on the test set, is referred to as the aleatoric error. When comparing results for different weight initializations (seeds), an epistemic ensemble error must be considered, which is also estimated from the standard error. The aleatoric error mostly depends on the size of the test set, while the quality of the estimated ensemble error is constrained by the limited amount of samples per ensemble.

One major source of difficulty in training is the size of the dataset. Since training has to be performed many times, the training speed of the architectures has to be significantly increased. This is achieved by changing the data format to that of numpy's memory-mapped files\footnote{See e.g. https://numpy.org/doc/stable/reference/generated/numpy.memmap.html.} and implementing sequential data loading. Highest training speeds are achieved when using a self-written implementation of multiprocessing.

\subsection{Estimates for models of the neutral hydrogen fraction}
During inference of the neutral hydrogen fraction, when evaluating CE and MI on models based on the $5\%$ rule, the expected behaviour of these estimates is in most parts recovered: CE and MI increase with larger bottleneck dimension $N_B$, with the exception of an outlier model for $N_B=8$. The other selection rules do not show this behaviour. 
\subsubsection*{Training the model}
The average training time for this model is $22.3~\si{h}$, with no significant deviation for varying bottleneck dimensions. The bottleneck dimensions analyzed are $N_B \in \{4,6,8,15,16,25,30\}$, each as an ensemble for two or three different seeds. As an example, the training and validation losses for the ensemble with bottleneck dimension $N_B=15$ are shown in fig.~\ref{fig:train_val_bottle_15}. Vertical lines indicate the epoch chosen by best validation rule and dotted lines indicate the epoch chosen by the $5\%$ rule. The loss plots for all other ensembles can be found in Appendix~\ref{appendix3}.

    \centering

    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{graphics/ensemble_15/joint_losses_excluded.png}
    \label{fig:train_val_bottle_15}

In all cases, training and validation losses improve and continue to improve after overfitting begins. Soon thereafter the best validation loss is reached and all models then plateau in both training and validation loss. This behaviour is observed for all architectures trained on the neutral hydrogen fraction. It is important to note that training of the ensemble for $N_B=15$ is comparatively stable, with training and validation losses converging to very similar values across seeds. This indicates that the model reaches similar regions in the loss landscape, independent of the initialization. This is not the case for all models, and training for smaller bottlenecks in particular is less stable.

\subsubsection*{Comparing model selection rules}
In order to compare the model selection rules, table~\ref{tab:training_stability} summarizes the average amount of epochs it takes for a model to reach the epochs chosen by the $5\%$ and best validation selection rules. Choosing models by the $5\%$ rule results in a higher variance in the selected epoch for models with larger bottleneck dimensions, while choosing models by the best validation rule results in a higher variance in the selected epochs for models with smaller bottleneck dimensions. The $5\%$ selection rule imposes the strictest selection choice, while model choice by best validation performance allows the model to learn longer before making a CE/MI estimate. For both selection rules, architectures with $N_B=4$ train the longest before being chosen, but there is no strong pattern for the choice of epoch for the other $N_B$.

\subsubsection*{Estimates of conditional entropy and mutual information}
In order to visualize how conditional entropy and mutual information are determined for a specific model, fig.~\ref{fig:distr_q_log} shows a histogram plot of log conditional probabilities $\log q_\phi (\theta_i|s(x_i))$ as given by the flow, evaluated on the test set on a fiducial model, and fig.~\ref{fig:distr_q_log_comparison} shows the log probabilities from the separately trained prior model. The estimate of conditional entropy and its error is then the mean and standard error of all $\log q_\phi (\theta_i|z_i)$, and the estimate of mutual information is obtained in the same way once all $\log q_\psi(\theta_i)$ have been subtracted, as recorded in eq.~\ref{eq:final_ce_estimate} and eq.~\ref{eq:final_mi_estimate}, respectively. The results are marked in the figures. Due to the fact that some histogram plots of conditional probability show significant kurtosis, all error estimates in all analyzed models are confirmed by bootstrap analysis. The final results for all selection rules are now visualized. Fig.~\ref{fig:final_xHI_5_pct} displays final CE estimates using the $5\%$ selection rule. One can observe a general increase in the estimate of conditional entropy for larger bottleneck dimensions, with the exception of the models for $N_B=8$. The insignificant increase in conditional entropy between $N_B=25$ and $N_B=30$ points towards a saturation in the conditional entropy for higher bottleneck dimensions. An alternative interpretation is that the optimal summary is already found at bottleneck dimensions lower than $N_B=15$, as expected for the highly correlated neutral hydrogen fraction labels, though this fails to explain the behaviour of the estimate monotonously increasing for higher $N_B$.

In summary, the $5\%$ selection rule provides estimates of CE and MI with the smallest errors and behaviour closest to that expected. The other selection rules lead to larger errors and lower CE/MI estimates for models with a larger bottleneck dimension. However, using the $5\%$ selection rule the optimal bottleneck dimension cannot be resolved, since the best performing dimensions is $N_B=8$, but models with larger $N_B$ show in increasing trend in informativeness with increasing $N_B$. The last epoch selection is ruled out by returning the worst results.

\subsection{Estimates for models of the simulation parameters}
The 6-parameter inference problem is easier: the $5\%$ selection rule yields a final MI estimate that monotonously increases with bottleneck dimension $N_B$, with one exception likely due to training instability. The best validation rule yields smaller errors for some samples and an almost monotonously increasing MI estimate. The last epoch selection rule fails to reproduce these results. Since the prior distribution is known, the estimate of MI comes with no additional uncertainty towards the CE estimate. As such, in the following only MI estimates will be given.

\subsubsection*{Training the model}
The average training time for this model is $10.6~\si{h}$, with no significant difference across bottleneck dimensions. Since the CNF is a much lighter architecture than the CNN, the CNN pretraining and final finetuning stages both have a similar epoch time, while in the CNF pretraining stage, the outputs of the CNN can be reused across epochs. Since the processing of an entire image is much more expensive than that of the CNN output, the pretraining of the flow is accomplished in less than $10~\si{min}$ in all cases. This makes this training setup faster than that used for inference of neutral hydrogen.

The bottleneck dimensions analyzed are $N_B \in \{3,4,5,6,7,9\}$, each for two different seeds. As an example, the training and validation losses for an ensemble with bottleneck dimension $N_B=6$ are shown in fig.~\ref{fig:train_val_6_p_bottle_6}. The loss plots for all other ensembles can be found in Appendix~\ref{appendix4}. The first plot shows the CNN pretraining stage, during which the MSE error drops to around a third its initial value and begins plateauing. The CNF loss quickly drops during its pretraining and has reached a plateau when finetuning begins. Do to the new learning rates used for finetuning, the CNF loss might initially rise at the beginning of finetuning. However in all cases, the finetuning stage does eventually lead to a further improvement in CNF loss. For some $N_B$, a significant spike in training and validation losses is observed during finetuning, which leads to instability of results achieved with the $5\%$ selection rule.

\subsubsection*{Estimates of mutual information}
The estimates of conditional entropy are obtained in the same way as previously, and since the prior is known, there is no additional uncertainty in estimating mutual information. Hence only MI estimates are shown in this section. In order to compare ensemble and aleatoric errors, table~\ref{tab:final_mi_6_p_5_perc} shows the final MI estimates using the $5\%$ selection rule. The other tables can be found in Appendix~\ref{appendix2}. The results show the expected monotonous increase in MI, while the MI curve begins to saturate for higher $N_B$. The aleatoric error dominates, indicating higher training stability than for the inference of the neutral hydrogen fraction, likely due to the lower-dimensional parameter and bottleneck spaces. An exception is the model for $N_B=9$, where for one training run the $5\%$ rule is never fulfilled during finetuning. If discarding this run, the estimate of MI for $N_B=9$ is $\hat{\mathbb{I}}=(12.87 \pm 0.11)~\si{nats}$, further indicating that a saturated value of MI has been reached.
